### Post 2 — Wednesday (How-to)

**Hook:** How to write a chaser email that actually gets paid.

---

Most chaser emails fail for the same reason.

They're too apologetic.

"I just wanted to follow up on the below..."
"Sorry to chase again..."
"I hope you don't mind me reaching out..."

You're owed money. You don't need to apologise for asking for it.

Here's a framework that works:

**Line 1:** State the fact. Invoice number, amount, date due.
**Line 2:** One specific question. "Can you confirm when this will be processed?"
**Line 3:** One clear deadline. "We'd appreciate payment by [date]."
**Line 4:** One consequence. "If we don't hear from you by then, we'll need to escalate."

Short. Direct. No apology.

The tone that gets paid fastest is confident and professional — not aggressive, not apologetic.

What's your current chaser process? One email, or a sequence?

### Post 5 — Wednesday (How-to)

**Hook:** What to do when a debtor goes completely silent.

---

What to do when a debtor goes completely silent.

No replies to emails. No answer on the phone. Nothing.

This is the situation most internal credit control teams don't have a process for.

Here's what works:

**Step 1:** Change the channel. If you've been emailing, call. If you've been calling, write a physical letter.

**Step 2:** Change the sender. A message from the MD or a new contact name often breaks the silence.

**Step 3:** Acknowledge the silence directly. "We've made several attempts to reach you regarding invoice [X]. We need to hear from you by [date] to avoid escalating this matter."

**Step 4:** Set a deadline with a real consequence. "If we don't receive payment or a response by [date], we'll pass this to our external recovery team."

**Step 5:** Follow through. If you set a deadline, act on it.

The companies that don't pay are usually testing whether you'll actually escalate.

Most don't. That's why they don't pay.

### Post 8 — Wednesday (How-to)

**Hook:** How to price a contingency debt recovery engagement (so you know you're getting a fair deal).

---

If you're considering outsourcing debt recovery, here's how to evaluate what you're being offered.

The headline rate matters less than you think.

Here's what to check:

**1. Is there a setup fee?**
Some agencies charge £50–£100 per case just to load your debt. That's a cost regardless of outcome.

**2. Is it truly contingency?**
"No win no fee" sometimes has small print — monthly retainers, admin fees, case review charges.

**3. What's the minimum?**
Some agencies won't touch debts under £2,000. Others won't take a portfolio under £50,000.

**4. What's included in the rate?**
Find out whether the rate includes phone outreach, or just email. Some only do automated letters and the rate reflects that.

**5. What's the escalation path?**
If automated outreach fails, what happens next? Do they pass back to you, or does their process include personal calls?

A genuinely good contingency arrangement costs you nothing unless it works.

The market rate for commercial B2B debt is 10–25% depending on age and complexity. No setup fees is better than a low rate with hidden costs.

Any questions about what to look for? Happy to give a second opinion on any quotes you've received.

### Post 10 — Monday (Data)

**Hook:** The statute of limitations on a commercial debt in England and Wales is 6 years.

---

The statute of limitations on a commercial debt in England and Wales is 6 years.

After that, it becomes statute-barred and is legally unenforceable.

This is the fact that should make every finance director act earlier, not later.

Most aged debt we see falls into one of three categories:

1. **Genuinely disputed** — the debtor has a legitimate grievance. These need to be resolved, not chased.

2. **Administratively lost** — the debtor's AP team has the invoice but hasn't processed it. A structured approach recovers these quickly.

3. **Intentionally delayed** — the debtor is managing their cash flow at your expense. These respond to escalation and deadlines.

The mistake is treating all three the same way.

A good audit tells you which category each debt falls into before you spend time chasing.

If you've got invoices older than 12 months, what category are most of them in?

### Post 11 — Wednesday (How-to)

**Hook:** How to have the "we're passing this to our recovery team" conversation without losing the client.

---

The hardest conversation in credit control:

"We're escalating your invoice to our external recovery team."

Most agencies avoid it because they're afraid of losing the client relationship.

Here's how to handle it professionally.

**Frame it as a process, not a punishment:**
"We have a standard process for invoices over 90 days — at that point we pass them to our external partner. It's nothing personal and it doesn't affect our working relationship."

**Be direct, not apologetic:**
"Invoice [X] for £[amount] is now 94 days overdue. We need to resolve this. Our external team will be in touch within 48 hours."

**Keep the client relationship separate:**
The consultant relationship and the invoice are two different things. Your external partner handles the invoice. You handle the relationship.

**Follow up personally after resolution:**
Once the debt is settled, reach out to the client directly. Acknowledge it's been resolved and move forward. Most professional relationships survive this if handled calmly.

The agencies that handle this well retain most clients. The ones that avoid the conversation often lose both the money and the client.
